Christmas

The main purpose of celebrating Christmas is to commemorate the birth of Jesus Christ. Christians believe that Jesus is the son of God who came to Earth to save humanity from sin. Christmas is also a time to celebrate God’s love for us. Christmas is a time for giving and celebrating with loved ones. It is also a time to reflect on the past year and to look forward to the future. Christmas is a religious holiday for Christians, but it is also celebrated by many people of other faiths. For many, Christmas is a time to exchange gifts, decorate their homes, and enjoy festive meals with family and friends.

Easter

Without a doubt one of the most important celebrations in the Christian calendar, Easter is a holiday that celebrates the Resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ. After his crucifixion, death, and burial, three days later, He rose from the grave. By this, He conquered death and redeemed us from sin. According to dictionary.com, Easter is “an annual Christian festival in commemoration of the resurrection of Jesus Christ, observed on the first Sunday after the first full moon after the vernal equinox, as calculated according to tables based in Western churches on the Gregorian calendar and in Orthodox churches on the Julian calendar.

Epiphany

So, what exactly is Epiphany and why do we celebrate it? Epiphany is the perception of the essential nature or meaning of something. As previously described, we celebrate Epiphany to honor the Three Wise Men who sought Christ to acknowledge His birth, welcome Him with the most expensive gifts of the time, and reveal His coming. Because the holiday is celebrated in honor of the Magi, it has often been called the Twelfth Night (because it occurs 12 nights after Christmas) or Feast of the Three Kings.

Good Friday

Simply put, Good Friday is set aside for Christians to remember and mourn Jesus’s death. It was on this day that religious leaders arrested Jesus for claiming to be the son of God and King of the Jews, as the biblical story goes. The leaders sentenced Jesus to crucifixion, the highest form of criminal punishment at the time, for his teachings. Given its grim origins, calling this holiday “Good Friday” probably sounds counterintuitive.
